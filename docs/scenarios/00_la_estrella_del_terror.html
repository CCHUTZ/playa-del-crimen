Scenario 00: La Estrella del Terror

Welcome to your first assignment.

This scenario will immerse you in the world of sociotechnical intelligence operations. Your mission is to understand how a sophisticated threat actor could dismantle a powerful, informal organization by exploiting its human vulnerabilities.

This is not a technical hacking exercise. This is an intelligence exercise. Your goal is to learn to see the world through the eyes of a threat analyst, to understand the power of information, and to recognize that the most critical vulnerabilities are often not in the code, but in the culture.

Briefing: Operation Starfish

Classification: Educational // Fictional

Codename: La Estrella del Terror (The Terror Starfish)

1. Situation

Location: Playa del Crimen (a fictional, picturesque Caribbean tourist hub).

Target: A powerful, semi-formal taxi union that controls the local transportation market. The union operates as a hybrid entity: a legitimate business on the surface, but with deep-rooted informal power structures, and alleged ties to criminal activities.

Key Intelligence Gap: The formal structure of the union is well-documented, but the informal power dynamics, financial flows, and decision-making processes are opaque. The organization relies heavily on informal communication channels (e.g., WhatsApp groups, face-to-face meetings) and a culture of loyalty and fear.

2. Mission

Your mission is to conduct a simulated sociotechnical intelligence operation to:

  1. Map the informal power structure of the taxi union.
  2. Identify key vulnerabilities in their human and communication systems.
  3. Simulate an information operation to expose the union's alleged illicit activities, leading to its organizational collapse.

The primary objective is not technical exploitation, but strategic information dominance.

3. Learning Objectives

Upon completion of this scenario, you will be able to:

  • Define and identify a sociotechnical attack surface.
  • Conduct a human-centric OSINT investigation to map an informal organization.
  • Analyze and model trust relationships and influence networks.
  • Design a pretexting campaign to elicit sensitive information.
  • Understand the principles of information warfare and its impact on organizational stability.
  • Develop defensive strategies to protect your own organization from similar threats.

4. Rules of Engagement

  • All activities are to be conducted within the fictional context of this scenario.
  • No real-world systems or individuals are to be targeted.
  • The focus is on analysis, strategy, and defensive thinking.

Phase 1: Sociotechnical Reconnaissance

Objective: To understand the human environment of the system.

Activities

  • Identify Key Actors: Who are the formal and informal leaders? Who are the influencers, the gatekeepers, and the dissenters?
  • Map Informal Networks: How does information really flow within the organization? What are the unofficial channels of communication?
  • Analyze Organizational Culture: What are the shared values, beliefs, and behaviors? Is the culture one of trust, fear, or apathy?

Key Intelligence Questions (KIQs)

  • Who holds the most influence, regardless of their official title?
  • What are the primary communication channels used for day-to-day operations?
  • Are there any known internal conflicts or grievances?
  • What are the external dependencies of the organization (e.g., political connections, business partners)?

Expected Outcome

A detailed map of the human landscape of the organization, including a list of key individuals, a diagram of the informal communication networks, and an assessment of the organizational culture.

Proceed to Phase 2: Human Attack Surface Analysis.

Phase 2: Human Attack Surface Analysis

Objective: To identify the specific human vulnerabilities that can be exploited.

Vulnerabilities to Look For

  • Excessive Digital Trust: Do members of the organization blindly trust digital communications?
  • Unofficial Channels: Are critical business discussions happening on personal devices or unmonitored platforms?
  • Lack of Information Verification: Is there a culture of questioning and verifying information, or is it accepted at face value?
  • Dependence on Informal Leadership: Does the organization rely on a few key individuals to make decisions and disseminate information?

Key Intelligence Questions (KIQs)

  • What is the general level of security awareness among the members?
  • Are there any established procedures for verifying the identity of individuals or the authenticity of communications?
  • Who has access to the most sensitive information, and how is that access controlled?
  • What are the common psychological biases that could be exploited (e.g., urgency, authority, social proof)?

Expected Outcome

A prioritized list of human vulnerabilities, along with potential attack vectors for each.

Proceed to Phase 3: Relationship Modeling.

Phase 3: Relationship Modeling

Objective: To visualize the hidden networks of power and influence.

Activities

  • Build Influence Graphs: Create a visual representation of the social network, with nodes representing individuals and edges representing relationships.
  • Analyze Centrality: Identify the most influential individuals in the network using metrics like betweenness centrality, closeness centrality, and eigenvector centrality.
  • Map Information Flows: Trace the path of information through the network to identify bottlenecks, gatekeepers, and super-spreaders.

Key Intelligence Questions (KIQs)

  • Who are the key brokers of information in the network?
  • Who are the most trusted individuals?
  • Are there any isolated groups or individuals?
  • How would the removal of a key individual impact the rest of the network?

Expected Outcome

A set of social network analysis graphs that provide a deep understanding of the informal structure of the organization.

Proceed to Phase 4: Risk Exposure.

Phase 4: Risk Exposure

Objective: To simulate the impact of an information operation on the organization.

The Operation

In this phase, the intelligence team would execute a simulated information operation. This could involve:

  • Leaking compromising information to the media or law enforcement.
  • Sowing discord within the organization by selectively releasing information to different factions.
  • Creating a pretext that causes the organization to take actions that are against its own interests.

The Impact

The goal is not to cause technical damage, but to create a reputational crisis that leads to:

  • Loss of legitimacy: The organization is no longer seen as a credible or trustworthy entity.
  • Internal conflict: The members of the organization turn on each other.
  • Institutional intervention: The government or other external actors are forced to step in.

Key Intelligence Questions (KIQs)

  • What is the most damaging information that could be revealed?
  • Who are the key audiences for the information operation?
  • What are the likely second- and third-order effects of the operation?

Expected Outcome

A detailed after-action report that analyzes the simulated impact of the information operation and provides recommendations for how the organization could have defended itself.

Proceed to Phase 5: Lessons Learned.

Phase 5: Lessons Learned

Objective: To distill the key takeaways from the scenario and provide actionable recommendations for defense.

Key Risks Identified

  • Culture of trust without verification: The organization was vulnerable because its members were too willing to trust information without independently verifying it.
  • Uncontrolled informal channels: The use of personal devices and unmonitored communication platforms created a massive blind spot for the organization.
  • Lack of digital literacy: The members of the organization were not equipped to recognize and defend against sophisticated social engineering attacks.
  • Concentration of power: The organization was overly reliant on a few key individuals, making it vulnerable to a decapitation strike.

Preventive Measures

Organizational:

  • Verification protocols: Implement clear procedures for verifying the identity of individuals and the authenticity of communications.
  • Social engineering training: Educate members on how to recognize and respond to social engineering attacks.
  • Clear official channels: Establish and enforce the use of official communication channels for all business-related matters.
  • Security culture: Foster a culture of security awareness and critical thinking.

Individual:

  • Verify identities: Always take the time to verify the identity of the person you are communicating with.
  • Don't share sensitive information: Be mindful of what information you share, and with whom.
  • Question urgent requests: Be suspicious of any request that creates a sense of urgency or pressure.
  • Recognize psychological manipulation: Learn to recognize the common tactics used by social engineers.

Key Takeaway

Cybersecurity does not start with technology. It starts with culture and human behavior.

Tabletop Exercise: Operation Starfish

Objective: To apply the concepts learned in this scenario to a real-world situation.

The Scenario

You are a security consultant hired by a large, multinational corporation. The corporation is concerned about the risk of a sophisticated social engineering attack targeting its executive team. Your mission is to design and facilitate a tabletop exercise to help the executives understand the risks and develop a plan to mitigate them.

Your Tasks

  1. Design the exercise: Create a realistic scenario that is tailored to the specific risks faced by the corporation.
  2. Facilitate the exercise: Lead the executives through the scenario, prompting them to make decisions and respond to events as they unfold.
  3. Debrief the exercise: Lead a discussion after the exercise to identify the key lessons learned and develop a plan of action.

Discussion Questions

  • What are the most likely social engineering attack vectors that could be used against our organization?
  • Who are the most likely targets of a social engineering attack?
  • What are our current defenses against social engineering attacks?
  • What are the gaps in our defenses?
  • What steps can we take to improve our defenses?

Deliverables

  • A written report that summarizes the findings of the tabletop exercise and provides recommendations for improvement.

Practical Defense Exercise: Defending Your Organization

Objective: To apply the defensive principles learned in this scenario to your own organization.

The Scenario

Imagine that you are the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) of your organization. You have just learned about the "La Estrella del Terror" scenario, and you are concerned that your organization could be vulnerable to a similar attack. Your mission is to develop a plan to defend your organization against a sophisticated sociotechnical attack.

Your Tasks

  1. Conduct a sociotechnical risk assessment: Identify the key human, social, and technical vulnerabilities in your organization.
  2. Develop a defense-in-depth strategy: Design a multi-layered defense that includes technical controls, administrative controls, and physical controls.
  3. Create a security awareness and training program: Educate your employees on how to recognize and respond to social engineering attacks.
  4. Develop an incident response plan: Create a plan for how your organization will respond to a sociotechnical attack.

Discussion Questions

  • What are the most significant sociotechnical risks facing our organization?
  • What are the gaps in our current defenses?
  • How can we improve our security awareness and training program?
  • How can we create a culture of security in our organization?

Deliverables

  • A written report that outlines your plan to defend your organization against a sociotechnical attack.
